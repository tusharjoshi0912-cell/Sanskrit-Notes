A Glimpse of VEDIC LITERATURE

by Kumar Sanjay Jha

Overview

This article is an attempt to present the fundamental texts in what constitutes Vedic Literature. It is a road mark for those who want to set out the explore the depths of the Vedas. The term 'Vedic literature' simply means literature based on or derived from the Vedas. The texts which constitute the Vedic literature are: 1. The four Vedas i.e. Samhitas, 2. the Brahmanas attached to each of the Samhitas, 3. the Aranyakas, and 4. the Upanishads.

The Vedas: The Foundation

The Rg-Veda, Samveda and Yajurveda are collectively known as Vedatrayi. Atharva Veda is considered a later addition. Bharatamuni's Natyasastra is considered to be the panchama Veda (fifth Veda).

1. Rg-Veda (Book of Hymns)

The Rg-Veda is the oldest among all Vedas. It must have been compiled over several centuries, with some of the earliest hymns composed around 1500 BC. Passed on orally from teacher to disciple, its different parts (mandalas) are ascribed to different families of seers. It is the foundation of all Vedic literature and is mainly a collection of hymns by priestly families, recited during sacrifices. The Rg-Veda contains 1028 hymns (sukta) divided into ten mandalas. Mandalas 2 to 7 are considered the earliest. The tenth mandala, a later addition, contains the famous Purushasukta, explaining the origin of the four varnas (castes). The universally famous Gayatri mantra (Savitri) is in the Rg-Veda. There are five divisions of Rg-Veda: Sakala, Baskala, Asvalayana, Sankhayana and Mandukya. The total mantras are 10,600.

2. Samaveda (Book of Melodies)

Derived from the root saman ('melody'), this Veda is a 'collection of melodies'. It consists of 1603 verses (with all but 75 borrowed from the Rg-Veda), which were meant to be sung at the time of the Soma sacrifice. The Samaveda is important in tracing the history of Indian music.

3. Yajurveda (Book of Sacrificial Prayers)

The Yajurveda is the book of the Adhvaryu priests, prescribing procedures for performing sacrifices. It has two main texts: Sukla Yajurveda (or Vajasaneyi) and Krsna-Yajurveda. It contains short magic spells used by priests during sacrifices. The grammarian Patanjali refers to 101 schools of Yajurveda.

4. Atharvaveda (Book of Spells & Charms)

Entirely different from the other three, the Atharvaveda is the latest of the four and for a long time was not included as a Veda. It describes the popular beliefs and superstitions of the humble folk, with hymns used to ward off evil spirits. It is divided into 20 kandas, has 711 hymns, and a collection of 5987 mantras. Its two divisions are Paippalada and Saunakiya.

Commentaries & Philosophy

The Brahmanas (Ritual Texts)

The Brahmanas explain the hymns of the Vedas. They are written in prose and elaborately describe sacrifices and their mystic meanings. The Rg-Veda has the Aitareya and Kausitaki Brahmanas, composed by Hotri-priests. The Sukla Yajurveda is appended with the Satapatha Brahmana ('one hundred sacred paths'), which is the most exhaustive and important of all. The Atharvaveda has the Gopatha Brahmana.

The Aranyakas (Forest Books)

The word Aranyaka means 'the forest'. These 'forest books' were for hermits and students in the jungles and are concluding portions of the Brahmanas. They were to be studied in the forest under a strict code of secrecy. Dealing with mysticism and symbolism, they offer the bridge between Karma marga (way of deeds) and the jnana marga (way of knowledge).

The Upanishads: The Vedanta

The word Upanishad has been derived from the root Upa-ni-sad that means 'to sit down near someone'. It denotes a student sitting under the feet of his guru to learn. Eventually the word began to be used for the secret knowledge imparted by the guru to his selected pupils. Today the word is associated with philosophical knowledge and spiritual learning. Upanishads are also called Vedanta (the end of the Veda) firstly, because they denote the last phase of the Vedic period and secondly, because they reveal the final aim of the Veda. Our nation's motto Satyameva jayate is taken from the Mundakopanishad.

In fact, the Upanishads are the culmination of ancient Indian philosophical ideas. The whole of later philosophy of India is rooted in the Upanishads. The philosophical principles of Sankara, Ramanuja, Ramakrishna Paramahans, Aurobindo and others are derived from Upanishads. There are 108 Upanishads classified according to the Vedas, and were composed by several learned saints between 800 BC and 500 BC. Some of the most renowned Upanishads are Aitareya, Kausitaki, Taittariya, Brhadaranyaka, Chhandogya and Kena. All these are older than Buddha and Panini. Among the later Upanishads mention may be made of Katha, Svetashvatara, Isa, Maitrayaniya etc., which lay stress on Samkhya and Yoga doctrines. The language of these Upanishads is classical Sanskrit and not the Vedic Sanskrit.

Like Aranyakas, the Upanishads also give less importance to ceremonies and sacrifices. They discuss various theories on the creation of the Universe and define the doctrine of Karma (action). Brahman (God) and atman (soul) are treated as identical. They profess the goal of life to be-to attain moksha (salvation) which is possible through meditation and self-control.

All the works referred to above are also called Sruti which means 'revelation'. Literally speaking Sruti means 'heard'. The Vedas are not the outcome of logical interpretation, not a historical anecdote, but they are Divine revelations. The Vedic literature enjoys the status of the foremost authority in all religious matters for the Hindus.

Auxiliary Literature

Smritis are the auxiliary treatises of the Vedas and are the law books of Indian society. Literally 'Smrti' means 'to remember' and these are regarded, as a part of the revelation though not included in the Vedic literature proper.

The Six Vedangas (Limbs of Veda)

  • Siksha: deals with pronunciation.
  • Kalpa: deals with rituals.
  • Vyakarana: deals with grammar.
  • Nirukta: deals with etymology.
  • Chhanda: deals with meter.
  • Jyotisa: deals with astrosciences.

The Six Darshanas (Schools of Philosophy)

Similarly, there are six schools of Indian philosophy known as Shad-Darshana. These are written in Sutra (aphorism) style, which are short, to the point and without doubts.

  • Nyaya Darshana by Gautama
  • Vaishesika Darshana by Kanada
  • Sankhya by Kapila
  • Yoga by Patanjali
  • Purvamimansa Darshana by Jaimini
  • Uttaramimansa Darshana by Bhadarayana

The Four Upavedas (Applied Knowledge)

Both the Vedangas and the Darshanas claim to have derived inspiration from the Vedas the explain the Vedic Philosophy. Still they are not included in the umbrella of core Vedic literature. In the same manner, four Upavedas namely Dhanurveda (deals with the art of warfare), the Gandharvaveda (deals with the music), Silpaveda (deals with are and architecture) and Ayurveda (deals with medicine) also do not form part of the Vedic literature though they enjoy great sanctity.

Legacy of the Vedas

The Vedic texts have been nourished, simplified and continued from one generation to the next through the Sruti parampara by the great saint scholars and sages, to name a few Sri Sankaracarya, Caitanya Mahaprabhu, Madhvacharya and Ramanuja. The Vedas are the treasure housed of knowledge not merely spiritual, but scientific, cultural and material. A fresh look at them, overcoming the hitherto laden emphasis on their religiosity would render that wisdom to us.

Concept of the Mind in Psychology

In the 18th century, psychologists used the introspective method to observe the mind, but it was not reliable for building a general model. So unobservable mental concepts were put aside during the behaviorist period, which focused only on observable behavior. They focused on observable behavior and ignored the mind, but behavior is different from person to person. That's why this method was rejected.

Then, cognitive psychology came into existence, studying the mind using experiments. There are two methods:

  • Mind as information processing - that is cognitive science.
  • Mind connected to brain - that is neuroscience.

Functions of Cognitive Models

Cognitive psychology studied how stimuli lead to responses. They found the mind processes information like attention, making decisions, recognizing differences.

The mind processes information in steps like senses, sensory memory, short-term memory, long-term memory, and responses. There are two types of processes: Automatic shallow processing in the unconscious mind, and deep awareness and emotion.

Cognitive Theory and Emotion

In cognitive theory, some argue emotions depend on cognitive evaluations, in attributing a meaning to events. On the other side, cognitive evaluation depends on deeper interior undertones of conscious affect, which are more powerful contributors to mental behavior than cognitive thinking.

The inner region of the mind includes emotions, feelings, intuitions, and inner sense of self, which are important for decision making, learning, and motivation.

Summary

In short, the mind works at many levels, from automatic actions to deep yearnings. Our thoughts help us to learn and grow.

The Vedic Model of Mind

The Vedic model of the mind explains the relationship between Prakriti (nature) and Purusha (the self). From this relationship, various mental and physical components emerge.

The Vedic Levels of Mind (The Koshas)

In the Vedas, it is said that our mind is covered by five Koshas. These are five levels of mind, which are called sheaths or layers.

The Five Koshas

Annamaya Kosha: The most expressed and grossest sheath associated with the physical form. It is the body made of food and is considered matter stuff, related to sensory experiences.

Pranamaya Kosha: Non-physical, associated with vital breath and energy. It has an inward and outward pulsation and connects universal energy to individual energy. It represents subtle matter.

Manomaya Kosha: Associated with the thinking mind and mental impressions. Intelligence is expressed in the form of thoughts. This is where the mind stuff resides.

Vijnanamaya or Gyanamaya Kosha: Associated with knowledge, wisdom, and intelligence. It helps in understanding and differentiating subtle things. It represents intelligence stuff and helps us experience the duality of a subjective mind and an objective mind, as well as the temporary and the permanent.

Anandamaya Kosha: The innermost level. It is a balanced state of the three fundamental qualities (gunas): sattva, rajas, and tamas. Associated with a blissful body, peace, and pure consciousness. It connects ourselves with the universal self, which is Purusha.

25 Levels in Sankhya Philosophy

The Sankhya philosophy explains 25 levels of our body and mind:

  • 5 Gross Elements (Mahabhutas)
  • 5 Subtle Elements (Tanmatras)
  • 5 Action Organs (Karmendriyas)
  • 5 Sense Organs (Jnanendriyas)
  • Manas (Thinking)
  • Mahat (Intellect)
  • Ahamkar (Ego)
  • Prakriti (Universal Nature)
  • Purusha (Pure Consciousness)

Mind According to Vedas

Modern psychology uses objective research to study sensory input in the brain, but it is hard to study deeper levels from the outside. We cannot understand such concepts without understanding our own consciousness. All the researchers are shallow, not that much deeper to understand thoughts and senses.

Manas (Mind)

All inner subjective functions are controlled by the mind. The body is the physical system. The term mind is different from senses, intellect, and the individual self. Manas refers to the cognitive level of thinking, analyzing, and reasoning. This thinking mind guides the senses and organs of action. There is a co-awareness between the thinking mind, directing the inputs of senses, directing the actions, organs and outer behavior.

Sometimes we use the phrase "screen of the mind" which is used to describe a part of the mental process. It unites perception, thoughts, feelings, and memories. This screen of the mind isn't responsible for dreams during sleep, which come from a deeper level of our mind. It is like our inner movie screen. When we see something, it remains in our memory and creates a clear picture in our mind. Then we can see them on the screen of the mind, and when we sleep, in our dreams.

Intellect (Buddhi)

The deeper decision-making and problem-solving function is associated with the term intellect, also called Buddhi. The deeper discriminative functions of the intellect become active and extend into a deeper level when a more profound level of the thinking mind works on solving problems and taking decisions. It also helps us to understand what is right and what is wrong.

Discrimination

The mind has two parts: dividing and unifying.

  • Dividing:

    means separating and analyzing ideas, concepts, and categories. This function is associated with the thinking mind and is linked to Manas, breaking things into categories and analyzing differences. For example, if there are two colors, red and green, understanding them as two different colors.

  • Unifying:

    means bringing things together into a whole, forming new models and world views. This function is associated with the feeling mind and is linked to Ahamkara, bringing things together and seeing connections to create new ideas and models. For example, considering red and green as colors (a group of colors).

Ahamkara (Deciding & Feeling)

This is the sense of "I" or Ego. Thinking processes are underlain by deciding and feeling. This even deeper level, combining decision-making powers and feelings, can be associated with the process of discrimination. It also includes fine feelings from the heart (psychological heart), which gives us the energy and will to act. It also expresses mental intentions. This function helps us to think clearly and precisely, so we can find subtle differences between things. The feeling function helps us to integrate, harmonize, and unite different ideas and feelings.

Manas (the thinking faculty) is about setting boundaries, clarifying, separating, and specifying differences, creating categories, dividing and restricting. And the intellect (the feeling faculty) is about uniting, harmonizing, integrating, and making connections, making a sense of the whole rather than focusing on differences.

Individual Being (Mahat)

Mahat is your individual self. It is associated with uniting the self into a whole and separating it from everything else. It is connected to the mind, intellect, and senses. In Sankhya philosophy, the subtlest impulse of nature becomes Mahat and Ahamkara. This level integrates the senses, mind, and intellect into a unitary individual experience.

It is the deep feeling, "I feel, I think, I perceive, I act." Here, "I" focuses on three levels: thinking, feeling, and self-ego. The levels are different from each other but interconnected. Ego is not about selfishness or arrogance; it is about the feeling of being a separate person. When you say "I," it means you pull inward (to connect, love, help, and unite) or push outward (to separate, fear, and reject). It causes either giving or taking, attachment or non-attachment, love or fear, connection or separation. So all your feelings, actions, and being an individual come from these two forces. The desire to unite with everything and the desire to remain separate from everything—all your life experiences are the results of these two forces.

The Individual Soul (Jiva)

The inner organs of action, senses, thinking mind, intellect, ego, and consciousness together form the individual soul, or Jiva. This Jiva can never be destroyed; it can reach a higher universal level when fully developed. The infinite self (which means Purusha and Prakriti) expresses themselves as many individual selves (which means Jiva). These Jiva can be further expressed via additional initiations into other levels of the inner dimensions, which are intellect, mind, and senses.

The process now reaches the level of discrimination of the self (that means Ahamkara), passes through the observer Manas (the thinking faculty), and involves the process of observations conducted by the senses and the objects of the senses. Then the organs of action act on the qualities like sattva, rajas, and tamas, which also play an important role in this whole process of the creation, maintenance, and destruction of the universe and our body.

Mind According to Bhagavad Gita

The mind is where feelings happen. Feelings are higher than senses, but our senses control our feelings. So for a successful spiritual experience, the senses must be subdued and our feelings must be sufficiently good and strong.

We can control our restless and uncontrollable mind through spiritual practice. According to the 6th chapter, Lord Krishna gives an explanation about the methods to control the mind.

In the 33rd and 34th shlokas of chapter 6, Arjuna asked Shree Krishna that the mind is like a wind which is hard to control. He asked him to explain how we can control such a restless and uncontrollable mind. Shree Krishna gives Arjuna the tools and techniques to control the mind. In this chapter, he said it is the tendency of the mind that it is always restless and uncontrollable. We should work hard on practice (abhyasa) and non-attachment (Vairagya). Practice means regular efforts to bring the mind back to the focus, and non-attachment means not getting emotionally tied to desires and outcomes.

Why we can't control our mind?

We have become a slave of our mind. We are unable to change our old habits. Regular practice leads to drawing it towards someone who is in peace, and that is the goal. We also need to control our mind from running after those objects to which it has been attached.

What should we do to control our mind?

We should follow the another way Shree Krishna suggested, which is non-attachment. Tell your mind that you are the soul and at the top level and you are the boss.

Such attachments to the objects which give temporary happiness are the evils, and your mind always wanders around such evil objects. So ask your mind to remain unattached to such worldly objects and become still. Such attachments give more pain than pleasure.

Is there any any sulattan not based on selfishness?

Remind your mind about all the sufferings you get due to attachment. Slowly, slowly try to regain your mind from attachments. With regular practice, after some time your mind will surely feel free from attachments, and then it becomes easy to control the mind.

Essential Nature of The Human Mind

As mentioned in the 1st shloka of chapter 7, the mind is referred to as the sixth sense. It is a person's faculty of thoughts and his ability to remember things. The mind is the center of ideas, opinions, perceptions, emotions, will, etc. One's thoughts are rooted in his past experience and likely future events. The mind can make or break a person. It has power whether for good or evil; it can help us to progress or cause our downfall. It is restless and always running after one or another object. So in this chapter, it is compared with a drunk monkey.

As stated in shloka 7.4 and 7.5, the mind belongs to the material and not to consciousness. According to shloka 13.1 to 13.7, the mind is like the field of activities; all changes like birth, growth, and death take place in this field. It is the doer of all activities and the self (individual self) is the witness of all such activities. Thus, it belongs to material nature, not pure consciousness. The mind is above the senses but below intelligence and the self. The nature of the mind has been described by Arjuna in shloka 6.33 to 6.34 as unsteady, restless, turbulent, unpredictable, variable and difficult to control.

The mind connects with the outside world through the senses. As stated in shloka 2.67, even one of the senses can carry away a person's wandering mind and intelligence if his mind is constantly engaged with that sense. Attachment with different sense objects creates a web of selfish desires in which the mind gets entangled, so it always remains restless. Even if the desires are fulfilled, one cannot get permanent happiness. New desires arise and prevent peace of mind. A continuously restless mind leads to misery, anger, etc. So many conflicts arising in the mind result in an unhealthy, confused, and disturbed mind. Due to this, fear, lust, greed arise, which create turbulence. In such a state, the mind cannot take the right decision at the right time. Engagement and attachment with the objects of the senses also cloud one's power of judgment.

The mind holds on to its views even if they are wrong and harmful. According to Arjuna, the mind is hard to control, even harder than controlling the wind. He expressed his helplessness in managing emotions, but with practice (abhyasa) and non-attachment (vairagya), it can be controlled. Instead of focusing on the outside world, the mind should be focused on the divine within.

In shloka 6.36, Krishna stated it was not easy to attain evenness of mind for an undisciplined person. The one who is self-controlled and makes sincere efforts through the right means can attain it.

The Mind is Fickle

The Bhagavad Gita says self-realization and control over the mind is necessary, but it is not possible without the stability of the mind. The mind is linked to the six senses and is fickle and unstable by nature. Stability means to remain calm in all situations, not getting overly happy or overly sad, staying focused and balanced. The stability of mind is necessary for inner peace, clarity, and spiritual growth.

Mind Needs to Be Stabilized

The state of the stability of mind is called "Sthitaprajna". When a person gives up all his desires and thoughts, his inner self is satisfied with himself. He stays calm in happiness, fear, anger, emotions, lust, etc. He does not get disturbed by praise or blame, good or bad events. A person who is stable in mind does not get overly happy when desires are fulfilled or upset when unpleasant things happen. He remains blissful, disconnecting himself from all attachments and staying connected with the Supreme within himself. Such a self is called a Yogi.

According to chapter 6, for such a Sthitaprajna who has controlled his mind, he has the best relation with the self, but at the same time, an uncontrollable mind is the enemy of the self. Sthitaprajna remains stable in cold or heat, happiness or sorrow, respect or disrespect, and always enjoys the company of the Supreme. He gets the knowledge of wisdom. The pieces of gold or pebbles or stone are all equal for him. He is superior who maintains the same attitude towards his well-wishers, friends, enemies, relatives, saints, sinners, etc.

Senses make the mind restless. Senses like eyes, ears, skin, nose, and tongue pull the mind towards the objects through desires. This constant craving for objects makes the mind restless and unstable. The Bhagavad Gita says if we control our mind through abhyasa and vairagya, the mind can be stable. For this, we should withdraw our senses completely from the objects, like a tortoise withdraws its limbs. This is the sign of self-mastery and self-control. Slowly, with practice, we can be able to control our mind and senses, so the disturbance in the mind reduces.

The desires remain inside until we reach spiritual realization. Peace comes only with the spiritual experience. The wise man with discrimination (the knowledge of right and wrong) can be disturbed by uncontrollable senses. Hence, controlling senses is essential to control the mind through which we can focus on the goal, the Supreme truth. At that time, the mind becomes stable. Such a stable mind leads to peace, intelligence, and spiritual progress.

Yoga Techniques to Stabilize the Mind

A Yogi should sit in a fixed place at a fixed time, alone, self-controlled, desireless, without any sense of possessiveness. He should concentrate on his mind only. The place should be clean, and his asana should be covered with cloth, deer skin, or Kuska grass. The Asana should be neither too low nor too high. He should have single-pointed concentration, keeping his mind and all sensory activities under firm control and in a straight position. He should sit in an erect posture with a peaceful mind, fearlessly practicing Brahmacharya also. He should practice yoga for self-purification. His mind should be subdued and established in "Me" (Shree Krishna), considering "Me" (Shree Krishna) his ultimate goal. Practicing thus, by keeping and merging himself in "Me," a Yogi attains the highest peace and nirvana in this world.

A Yogi should not eat too much or too less. He should not be an excessive sleeper or excessively sleepless. The Yogi who controls his diet, sleep, and recreation appropriately, and performs all his work, can see his sorrow reducing. His mind becomes disciplined. When a disciplined mind is established in the self, one can easily subdue all his desires. He becomes like a lamp in a windless place. Nothing can disturb his controlled mind because now his mind is under the control of divine power and united with his inner self. At this state, the mind stops all its movements. It becomes quiet and stable.

The state in which a person finds unlimited happiness is not dependent on external objects but comes from within. This happiness and peace are beyond the senses; it is an inner spiritual experience. The intellect of the Yogi understands the higher truth of existence, and once he is established in this truth, he remains steady and does not get affected by worldly ups and downs. In short, when the mind is stilled in yoga, restlessness stops, the lower self connects with the higher self, one feels deep, unlimited inner happiness which is permanent, and the intellect becomes more powerful and remains in truth. He becomes free from all the bondages and attains salvation.

Concentrating the mind on the goal is the solution. In verses 11.7 and 11.8, Lord Krishna gives assurance, "I rescue them, O' Partha, whose minds are set upon Me, from the ocean of mortal samsara. Fix your mind on Me. Operate your intelligence, and then, without a doubt, you shall come to Me only." In chapter 17, verse 16, the definition of the austerity of the mind is mental satisfaction, gentleness, silence, self-control, and purification of thoughts. These are called the austerity of the mind.

The Upanishadic Concept of Mind

Upanishads are ancient Indian spiritual texts. Hindu ideas and Western ideas about the mind are totally different, so a direct comparison is not possible. Hindu thoughts cannot be explained on the basis of suppositions or parallelism because they are based on pratyaksha, which is totally different from others.

The ideas and concepts of the mind are deeply rooted in the past, and all philosophical reflections are ultimately grounded in the Vedas and Upanishads. The Upanishads were composed about 800 BC. The Sanskrit term manas refers to the mind. The word manas is etymologically identical with the Latin word 'Mens'. It is not exactly the same as the English word mind; it has its own deep and unique meaning.

The Upanishads used similes to explain how the mind works, especially during self-reflection. Mind and prana are interconnected. To explain this, the Upanishad uses the example of a bird tied to a string. A bird flutters in all directions but finds no food. Like this, our mind is restless, always here and there and runs after desires, but always remains connected to prana. Since the bird is tied, it must return to the same place. Likewise, the mind has to settle back at the source when it finds no stability elsewhere.

To explain the origin of the mind, it emerges from the essence of food through subtle processes, just like the body. The Upanishads have given another simile, of churning milk to make butter. The origin of the mind is compared to the butter rising from the milk during churning.

The Upanishads use poetic and practical images to explain the mind's movements and sources. These similes used in Hindu texts are beautiful and helpful, but they do not fully explain the complete meaning of the mind in accordance with Hindu philosophy. They give clues, but there is a deeper, more complex understanding behind them.

There is a classical conception in the Katha Upanishad which is copied with modifications and mentioned in later literature, including Buddhist literature. It is compared with reins in a chariot. The horses are the sense organs called Indriyas. The reins (mind) control the wild horses and also guide them. In Hinduism, the mind is not just a thinking faculty but a core part of the human experience deeply connected with senses, emotions, will, and creativity.

Characteristics of the Mind

The mind is procurer (अवरोधिनीनी), which means it gathers all experiences and then acts vigorously, both in a restless state and in a waking state. It is restless, wandering, strong, stiff, and difficult to manage and control.

According to Western views, thinking and meditating are the two main functions of the mind. However, Hindu views suggest this is just one part of what the mind does. In Hinduism, the mind is active, impulsive, and tied to desires and wishes.

Pure vs. Impure Mind

The mind is called pure or impure according to the domination and prevalence of one element at the expense of the other. A pure mind is considered good and praiseworthy, disconnected from passion, softness, rashness, and desires. Such a calm mind is called a pure mind. An impure mind is an example of emotions like anger and an aggressive element like desire, which is called Kama in Sanskrit.

The mind becomes aggressive or intense due to desires, which influence the mind, and sometimes the mind creates desires. The mind rushes and creates desires, and human behavior or emotions are heavily shaped by desires, either as cause or as effect. Simultaneously, the mind creates doubts, fears, sensory enjoyments, and dislikes, and the mind is responsible for initiating actions.

It shows movement, energy, and runs. The mind is also responsible for the root of rebirth and the root of the Samsara tree. It is able to control Indriyas, which means the inner power behind the senses, which is subtle. The Indriyas are like untrained or trained horses full of energy. A wise person can control the urges residing behind the various senses by means of the mind, like a charioteer who controls horses.

Such control is considered ideal and is praised both in Hinduism and in philosophies like Samkhya or Stoicism. Hindu thoughts emphasize that checking sense activities is not enough for perfection, because the mind possesses self-motion. It requires steadiness. Stopping the senses from the object is not the solution, but detaching from the desire behind them is necessary.

Desires are connected with external goals that are obtained through specific sense organs. If these desires are dominant, the actions become false or misleading, and according to the Bhagavad Gita, it leads to the ethical destruction of morality.

Thus far, we have considered the mind in its more or less impure form. It is like a part of a psycho-physical system where the mind and body work together. The mind influences the sense organs to act, put effort, and make decisions. It is described as more than or higher than the senses. This is not only because the mind is subtle and immaterial, but mainly for the reason that it mediates the knowledge and understanding of the self, i.e., Atman. After helping to relate to the Atman through thought and reflection, the true self helps to gain meditative focus i.e., Dhyana.

Nature of Thoughts

The Kaushitaki Upanishad conveys the impression that thoughts are an essential element, i.e., Bhuta. Thoughts are not fixed; they have mobility and are always in motion.

Desire is indirectly associated with thoughts and thinking in general. Even though the thought changes, the power remains. When we focus deeply on one desire, it becomes weak through Tapas (effort), Medha (intelligence), and Yoga (concentration).

Restless running after many objectives becomes calm and steady through Dhyana (meditation). This state of the mind is called quiescence (mental stability). The mind performs the duty of wishing, willing, and thinking, but a proper balance between them is necessary. The wise man who has achieved this balanced state and who remains the same—Sama—in any situation does not get carried away by emotions and laziness.

When he is in the state of Sama, he is in the state of 'doing for the sake of doing nothing,' which means staying calm and doing nothing. This balance or harmony is better than extremes. The Bhagavad Gita teaches us to develop this kind of calm and balanced mind.

In Hindu thoughts, the mind has a thought and an objective side (like a sensation) and a subjective side (like thoughts and feelings) with active and passive principles, respectively. This means these two are not in conflict but work together as active (doing) and passive (receiving) faculties.

Indriyas as the faculty of sensation, meditate on an external world through various sense organs. Indriyas bring the outer world's data. Thoughts convert these inputs or sensations into perceptions. Western psychology separates sensation and thoughts, but Hindu thought sees ideas or perceptions as stable and sensations as constantly changing. The Upanishad's view is not just about thoughts and senses like wishing and thinking, but also about 'Citta'. Citta has a special role in the mind. It is consciousness like the inner self or the storehouse of mental impressions. It has its own unique importance in understanding how the mind works. The mind can work properly only if it is balanced. The balance is between willing (desires) and thinking (reasoning). If there is not a balance between them, then:

  • The mind loses its objectivity.
  • It cannot connect inner thoughts with external facts, i.e., subjective with objective.
  • The judgments and actions lose the proper meaning of truth.

The text talks about manas, which helps us to believe, to opine, and to hold. These are internal functions like deciding and believing something to be true. If the mind is cut off from external input, it can create opinions that may be wrong, uncertain, or harmful.

Such an unbalanced mind makes assumptions and creates imaginations or false beliefs. Thus, the mind that only uses its own thoughts without facts can become confused or misleading.

Practice of Mind Control

Mind control is hard but achievable. Our mind affects us more than anything else. We always struggle to control it. Those who have already achieved control over their mind can truly help us. We can learn some simple and practical methods to manage our mind better from them. It is a very interesting inner game. If you have a sports person's attitude, you can enjoy it even during tough times or failures. To play this game, you need skills, alertness, goodness of heart, strategy, patience, humorous spirit, and courage. These qualities help you to stay strong in the worst circumstances.

Shree Krishna explains to Arjuna in the Bhagavad Gita about the supreme state of yoga, which means unity and self-mastery. About this high goal, Arjuna says in sadness, "to perform this yoga, which is characterized by the perfect evenness of mind, is difficult because the mind is restless, turbulent, powerful, and obstinate." Connecting it feels as hard as controlling the wind.

Shree Krishna listened to him and gave a reply important for all human beings of all times. He said, "Undoubtedly, O Arjuna, the mind is restless and hard to control, but by practice (Abhyasa) and non-attachment (Vairagya), it can be controlled."

From this conversation, we learn three basic things about mind control:

  1. It has not always been an extremely difficult task, even for a person of Arjuna's stature.
  2. It is possible to control the mind.
  3. There are some well-defined methods of controlling the mind.

Shree Krishna gave the whole secret of controlling the mind: practice (Abhyasa-Yoga) - training the mind again and again for the same thing, and detachment (Vairagya) - not being emotionally attached to things. It is the uniform verdict of all the saints of India down the ages that there is no other way of controlling the mind except through true practice and detachment, which is also called Abhyasa Yoga or the yoga of practice.

Dialogue between Shree Ramakrishna and a devotee

The story highlights the power of mental freedom and steady efforts. Shree Ramakrishna says, "Don't sit idle, don't stop, and don't become lazy because your spiritual consciousness has been awake a little. Keep moving, go forward. Beyond the forest of sandalwood, there are other and more valuable things, mines like silver mines, gold mines, and so on," which means there are higher truths beyond what you have seen. It is about deeper spiritual knowledge.

The devotee says, "But our legs are in chains. We cannot go forward."

Shree Ramakrishna replied, "What if the legs are chained? The important thing is the mind. Bondage is of the mind and freedom is also of the mind."

The devotee says, "But the mind is not under my control."

Shree Ramakrishna says, "How is that? There is such a thing as Abhyasa Yoga, which means yoga through practice. Keep up the practice and you will find that your mind will follow in whatever direction you lead it. The mind is like a white cloth. If a person just returned from a sandy road and you dip it in red dye, it will be red. If you dip it in blue, it will be blue. It will have whatever color you dip it in."

Abhyasa and Vairagya are no doubt the entire secret of controlling the mind, but how do we bring them into our self-stream? It is very important. We shall have to develop a strong will to control our mind. For that, we have to understand the nature of the mind. We have to learn certain techniques and practice them earnestly and intelligently.

Control of the Subconscious Mind

Conscious vs. Subconscious

It is hard to separate the conscious and subconscious parts of the mind, but for a practical understanding, one can separate them. Basically, the conscious mind actively thinks and the subconscious mind is hidden and automatic. The conscious mind decides and the subconscious mind stores deep habits and feelings.

If you want to control the mind on a conscious level, the efforts should start from controlling the subconscious. The subconscious influences our conscious thoughts and actions. Sometimes we all experience the inner conflict: we know what is right, but most of the time we fail to do it. We know something is wrong, but still, we do it. We make good decisions, set strong goals, and try to improve. But before we are aware of it, like the sand castle before a tidal wave, they are washed away. We stand confused and frustrated. We always make resolutions with our conscious part of the mind because this is the part we are aware of, but we ourselves break our resolutions with another part, the subconscious mind, which we don't know well. It is an unenlightened region of our mind.

The Challenge of Resistance

When we seriously try to control our mind, we face inner struggles, but the more we try, the harder it feels. First, we may wonder and ask ourselves, "Why am I getting worse day by day?" That sense is taking the decision seriously, but the truth is ignored. When we try to control our conscious mind, we face resistance from our subconscious mind. This resistance comes from whatever we think and do—past impressions and tendencies. These old impressions are stored deep inside us and try to come back into our awareness. If they don't match our current thinking, they are inconsistent, and we feel inner conflict.

The subconscious mind is like a cellar in a house. You don't know how much junk is there until you decide to clear it. As you begin, you are not sure what kind of things you will encounter. You face so many surprises and challenges. Soon, you get tired and leave the work unfinished. So it cannot become a clear and useful space. The same way, if we don't clear our subconscious mind, we cannot truly control our conscious thoughts. We must find ways of cleaning this dark region of the subconscious mind. But how can we do it?

Cleaning the Subconscious Mind

Suppose we want to clean an ink pot. How do we do it? We pour clean water into the pot. As the dry ink gets soaked, darker water comes out for some time, then less inky water comes out. At last, we don't find any traces of ink. Now, clean water poured into the pot causes clear water to come out of the pot. One of the ways of cleaning the subconscious mind is to pour holy thoughts into our mind and allow them to go deep down within ourselves. Holy thoughts are like pure water. We must not get frightened when we find dark water coming out. We must persist in pouring holy thoughts. A time will come when we shall find clear thoughts coming from within. This is how our subconscious mind can be cleared and controlled.

The Role of Karma and Spiritual Vision

We must not think that the subconscious mind is the storehouse of evil; it also stores our past good and noble deeds. It is a storage area of our experiences, beliefs, and feelings, which may be helpful or harmful in our efforts to control the mind. Our task should be to control and reduce harmful elements and utilize helpful elements. Shree Krishna assured Arjuna in the Bhagavad Gita that the yogi who stays as he is by being born again for the good work is stored up, and in his next birth, he is inherited with the intelligence acquired in his previous body.

This karma can be a powerful, unknown factor in this life's attempts at mind control. If we want real and lasting control over our mind, we must work on transforming the subconscious mind. We are not working on the highest goal of life, experiencing the super-conscious state. We can never be the master of the mind. As a result, both the conscious and subconscious minds remain restless. Only the experience of the highest spiritual vision of God can remove attachments, fears, likes and dislikes, illusions, mental disturbances, and sufferings.

Controlling the Mind

The Three Levels of Mind

Controlling the mind involves understanding its three main levels:

  • The subconscious mind: Our deep inner habits and actions.
  • The conscious mind: Our current thoughts and actions.
  • The superconscious mind: Our highest spiritual potential.

Swami Vivekananda reminds us that this is a huge task. We cannot focus only on what we are consciously aware of; we must also deal with our deep inner self and aspire to the highest spiritual awareness.

The Unconscious and Its Influence

The real effort must begin with working on the vast subconscious. Practical psychology focuses on the unconscious mind first because it is a storehouse of countless old conscious thoughts and actions, those we have now forgotten. These old impressions and habits don't disappear but get covered; we don't see them or think of them, but they are still there. This is compared to items left in a pocket—we may forget about them, but they still exist.

Some of these harmful impressions can slowly spoil or become worse if we don't deal with them. They affect both our good and bad actions without us even realizing it. The unconscious mind is a mixture of good and bad, but if these things are ignored for too long, some of them can be awakened and become harmful, just like a good seed left unattended. The unconscious mind causes many hidden problems in human life. The main goal is to attain self-awareness and control over the body and mind and all the automatic actions performed by inner organs within our bodies, such as the liver, which can be made to obey our commands.

The Journey to Superconsciousness

According to Swami Vivekananda, this is the first part of the study; there is more to do for spiritual development. The next step is to go beyond the conscious. Just as there is a world below consciousness (the unconscious), there is another world which is above consciousness (the superconscious state). When the superconscious state is achieved, a person becomes free and divine. Death turns into immortality, and one can overcome the fear and limitations of death. Weakness becomes power, and inner strength and spiritual energy increase. Bondages turn to liberty and freedom from all limitations. This state is described as infinity. The ultimate goal of human evolution, according to Vivekananda, is to achieve this state.

Yoga and Kundalini

To achieve this state, a yogi should do twofold work. First, he should learn how to control and work on the two energy channels in the body: Ida and Pingala. Ida is the left channel, and Pingala is the right channel. These are the two existing ordinary currents to control subconscious actions. Second, the yogi should learn to go beyond even consciousness towards a higher spiritual state. The true yogi, after a long practice of deep concentration, can attain the truth, and now the Sushumna current opens. The Sushumna is a central channel in the spine that opens; it normally remains closed. Now a current finds its way into it and gradually moves upward. The spiritual energy also moves upward, passes through different chakra centers, and reaches the brain. The yogi becomes fully conscious of his true self, which is nothing but God himself.

To awaken Kundalini through Raj Yoga instead of Pranayama is the process which helps to focus the mind. However, Pranayama should be learned personally from a qualified teacher because it is very powerful and subtle. As it is not easy to find such expert teachers, one should practice it gradually. But those who are disciplined, have been there, and are lucky to find a good teacher can easily achieve the control over the mind. With the guidance of teachers and regular practice, he can awaken all internal energies. Many people who live a worldly life are not able to practice Pranayama regularly. They can grow spiritually by simple methods like meditating on "Om" with faith. This regular practice with full concentration awakens Kundalini or hidden spiritual power. Shree Ramakrishna taught that reading books alone won't awaken spiritual consciousness. One should also pray to God sincerely. In a restless position, for a short time, Kundalini was roused by God. A disciple asked Swami, "How can the Kundalini be roused?" Swami replied, "Some people recommend certain physical or yogic exercises, but I believe that it can be best achieved through the repetition of the divine name and meditation. It is the most easiest spiritual exercise." Meditation must be done with the repetition of the mantra (sacred words). Thus, by the spiritual disciplines on the part of the devotee—repetition of the divine names, prayers, and meditation—our spiritual potency is awakened. This awakened power takes care of the difficulties of the subconscious mind. No one should be despairing, thinking that because he is not able to practice Raj Yoga disciplines, his way to control the subconscious mind is closed. One can repeat the divine name and continue with his practice of Japam, he will definitely get success.
